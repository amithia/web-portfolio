Atlas of Care

The problem

Paediatric triage in low-resource hospitals is time-critical and cognitively demanding. Clinicians across three hospitals in Sri Lanka were using paper-based protocols that required cross-referencing multiple documents under pressure. Decisions that should take two minutes were taking seven.

"By the time I'd found the right protocol page, the clinical picture had changed." — Consultant paediatrician, Colombo

My approach

I started with two weeks of contextual inquiry — shadowing clinicians on ward rounds, observing handovers, and interviewing nursing staff about failure points. Three patterns emerged: information was scattered, not surfaced; the visual hierarchy of existing forms didn't match decision priority; and digital tools that existed were designed for desktop, not mobile on a ward.

  • 22 hours of contextual observation across three wards
  • 12 in-depth interviews (consultants, registrars, nurses)
  • Workshop with clinical lead to map the decision tree
  • Heuristic audit of existing paper protocols

Design decisions

The core insight was that triage decisions follow a branching logic — not a checklist. I redesigned the interface around a guided branching flow rather than a form, surfacing only the next critical question at each step. Colour was used semantically (red = urgent, amber = watch, green = stable) with text labels to maintain accessibility under pressure.

Typography was sized for glanceable reading at arm's length. Tap targets were sized for gloved hands. The interface required zero onboarding — a clinician could use it without training.

Outcome

After a three-month pilot across three hospitals, average time-to-triage-decision dropped from 6.8 minutes to 4.1 minutes — a 40% reduction. Clinical accuracy scores (assessed by senior review) improved by 18%. The tool was adopted permanently by two hospitals and is being evaluated for national rollout.
